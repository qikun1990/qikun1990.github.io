    @spaceboy I absolutely hear you regarding the difficulty in making your money back on all the testing.

    Not gonna lie, it HAS become more difficult to make consistent profits from directing paid traffic to CPA offers. A few years ago, things were so easy anybody could do it. Now new affiliates actually need to do more work and be willing to INVEST for a while before being able to reap the rewards.

    One major trend I'm seeing is that newbies can no longer expect to get started with $1-2k. I'd say a minimum of $5k is needed just to have a decent chance at finding any sort of consistent profits.

    That "cycle" you're talking about does NOT have to be the same every single time! In fact, you need to constantly try to figure out how you can improve the process further. This is especially true if you're finding that your "cycle" or process isn't getting you results.

    As I've mentioned, there are basically 3 pillars to a pop campaign: Traffic, Landers and Offers. So, try to figure out all the opportunities for improvement for each pillar, and devote some budget to testing out each of them!

    You need to know WHY you're doing something a certain way, and question whether it's the best way. And if you're not sure, that means there's an opportunity for testing and potential improvement!

    Below are just a few examples of what you could be testing.


    OFFERS

    -Test more offer verticals. As a newbie, start with one of the more popular verticals like sweeps because you can easily find offers that will give you conversions, and conversions are what you need when you're learning the ropes. Another plus is that many/most networks would have offers for a vertical like sweeps. Check Adplexity Mobile to make sure that the vertical you choose is one of the major ones receiving a lot of traffic in your chosen geo - because then chances are the vertical is converting well in that geo.

    But once you know what you're doing, i.e. have a testing methodology, know how much to spend on testing an offer before calling it quits etc. - set aside a budget to test a new vertical every so often. There are less-popular verticals you won't even find on Adplexity until far down the page, that you can quietly make bank from. The audience would be less "blind" to the vertical because not as many affiliates are running it. Trouble is you may need to sign up to more affiliate networks because these less-popular verticals usually don't have as many offers available as sweeps for example.

    Do keep in mind that pop traffic is broad traffic, so only offers that have very broad appeal can work. Anything REMOTELY niche would be difficult - but I would love it if you would prove me wrong!

    Pop traffic is interruption marketing, people don't really have purchase intent or even the interest to fill out forms with more than a few fields.

    So: Extremely simple conversion flows, lead gen with a couple/few fields, free trials. Offers that have broad appeal. Everyone likes to win (sweeps), everyone wants to be entertained (gaming and video streaming), everyone wants to get rich quick (biz op, crypto, forex), everyone is scared their desktops/laptops/mobile devices would stop working (antivirus).

    -Test more affiliate networks. Different affiliate networks have different offers. As a newbie you may only be running with a couple of affiliate networks because you're following the 40-day tutorial, or because you got rejected by a few networks due to lack of proof of revenue and got discouraged, or you're afraid of cashflow issues from having revenue stuck on multiple networks all below payment threshold, or even because you're afraid of being hounded by multiple affiliate managers to run their offers.

    Once you're out of newbiehood, I would encourage you to explore more affiliate networks, including the smaller ones. You don't even HAVE to sign up for an account - get in touch with different networks just to have a conversation about their strongest verticals, best offers, and the offer conversion flow of those offers.

    To get in touch with affiliate network reps: Do searches in the skype directory on various aff network names, search linkedin, search the "contact us" sections of aff network websites. But the easiest place to start would be here: https://stmforum.com/affiliate-directory/

    And attending industry conferences such as Affiliate World and AdWorld to grow your contacts would be wise as well.

    Yes, with smaller networks you run the risk of nonpayment, but that's part of what you need to factor into your test budget. Avoid small networks with high payment thresholds (beyond say $100-200) and make sure they don't miss ANY payments. Stop traffic immediately if they miss one payment by even one day.

    Also: Once you find some success with a certain vertical, sign up to more affiliate networks that specialize in that vertical. Google is your friend. So is offervault.com. So is the STM affiliate directory I linked to above.

    -Minimize offer-testing budget. Use your best lander if you have one in the right language and offer type. Use the best placements/zones on your best traffic source. Test multiple offers in the same campaign if you can, because that way the inferior offers will likely get cut (via statistical significance) before the usual 10x payout budget. Actually, if you're already using your best placements/zones, you can likely get away with running only 5x payout to test an offer.


    LANDERS

    -Test more landers in the beginning. When you're running a type of offer for the first time, test more landers. I'd recommend 5-10 of the most popular lander styles and even some of the most popular variations of each style. And spend time to optimize them for speed. You need to be confident about your landers are good converters, or else you wouldn't know whether to blame your landers or offers for lack of conversions (as we've discussed).

    Good landers will usually continue to "work" for many weeks and months, unless banner ads which can reach fatigue in a matter of days. So it's worth it to test more landers in the beginning.

    And of course, once you've invested the money on testing and finding a good lander, be on constant lookup for more offers to test with it - you'd save so much time and money that way.

    -Innovate! Once you find a good offer, test more landers! Rewrite the wording or the entire sales angle, add/subtract lander elements and move them around, or even design landers completely from scratch. Examine landing pages from a different vertical than the one you're promoting, and 'borrow' angles/elements that you feel can also work for your vertical.

    -Get proper translations! In the beginning when you're mass-testing lots of geos with different languages and maybe even different verticals, paying for lander translations can get expensive. In that case, ripping landers that have 'received most traffic' on Adplexity should be fine.

    However, getting proper translations DOES have its merits. It's an easy way to have an advantage over the competition. A couple tips:

    ->Fiverr is good enough. OHT is fast but unnecessarily expensive IMO. Choose translators with lots of high ratings and you can't go far wrong.

    ->Tell them the text needs to be concise and "sales-y" because it's advertising text. Tell them if you get a good conversion rate you'll continue to use them in the future and pay more than their standard rates. Split-test multiple translators (by split-testing same lander multiple translations) and go with the one that gets you the best conversion rate.

    -Target more popular languages. In order to save money and time: Target a language that's spoken in several geos. That way you can prepare one set of landers to test offers in multiple geos. If you do that, consider getting proper lander translations done from the start, because it would be worth it.

    Having said THAT: More popular languages tend to be more competitive as well. So devote a part of your test budget (can be a smaller budget) on testing unpopular languages. Lots of affiliates wouldn't bother getting proper translations for unpopular languages so that's where you can potentially have an advantage.

    I'm kind of getting ahead of myself here. Please keep in mind that BEFORE you even choose lander language, you need to make sure there are enough good offers in geos that speak that language!

    -Experiment with scripts like backbutton and push collection. Doing so can squeeze extra revenue from the same traffic.


    TRAFFIC

    -Set up testing to find out how the traffic source algo works. Every time a question comes up in your head regarding targeting options, design a test that will allow you to find out! Remember the scientific method we all learned in highschool? Now would be a good time to use it!

    What kinds of testing am I talking about? This one by @twinaxe for example: https://stmforum.com/forum/showthrea...Type-Splittest

    Another example that comes to mind: Have you ever wondered whether bidding higher on the same placements/zones would make a difference in the conversion rate? Setting up a quick test will tell you!

    Start writing down these questions as they come to your mind, and dedicate a budget to test them! What you find out can really help all your future campaigns.

    Just a few general ideas:

    ->Test bids using different methodologies/approaches. Finding out how much to bid for which traffic segment will give you an edge, i.e. bid higher for better traffic and lower for not-as-good traffic.

    ->Test narrow targeting vs. broad targeting. (e.g. Often when you target broad you can get more traffic volume at cheaper prices; but by targeting narrow you can zoom in on the most profitable segments. Pros and cons - testing needed to tell which is better.)

    ->Test more ad formats. E.g. Most of us are running on PropellerAds, but how many have tested their Interstitials? I'm guilty of this - will do some testing there shortly.

    ->Test different cost models - CPM vs. SmartCPM vs. CPA. Each model involves different testing/optimization approach.

    ->Test different frequencies. 1/24 is the "safe" choice, but have you tried setting a lower bid for higher frequencies? Goes back to the "how much to bid for which traffic segment" I was talking about earlier.

    ->Test ad-block traffic. Hypothesis: Many affiliates may be excluding this traffic, so perhaps it can be bought for cheap? Maybe the conversion rate won't be very bad? Test it out!

    Major note: This type of experimentation shouldn't be limited to traffic source options! Extend the same thinking to tracker options and other tools - such as optimization tools like theoptimizer.

    -Test more traffic sources! When you have your first few profitable campaigns, don't even THINK about recuperating your investment - yet!

    Scale the campaign to 5-10+ new traffic networks! If you can find 1-3 that backs out for you, you've increased the potential amount of profits you could be making for all your future campaigns. Rinse and repeat.

    Your profits are limited by the amount of traffic volume you have access to. Invest in building blacklists/whitelists on traffic sources. Then keep looking for new+good offers to test in those same geos. That way you can save so much money and scale so much bigger, because you already have a proven lander and done cutting bad placements on multiple traffic sources.



    I'm just scratching the surface here because this is one post and not an entire tutorial, but hopefully I've made my point that Continuous Testing Leads to Continuous Improvement. Knowledge is indeed power. On top of your "real" campaigns, also set up some "test" campaigns to test your hypotheses and theories. The more testing you do, the more gold nuggets you pick up, the easier it will be for your future campaigns to succeed.

    Hope I've shed some light on your concern!

    Agree with Amy, you do not need to invest in design.

    I had a friend selling jewelry wholesale in the south of France 10/15 years ago. He was sourcing all products from China. He realized soon that the only thing that mattered to sell jewelry was a good story. So he was asking his suppliers to tell him a compelling story about why they made such a design for a piece, translating it into something French women would love, and sending this marketing material to his retailers, so that they were able to sell more easily to their clients. He made a killing doing that, while the other wholesalers were competing on pricing.

    Story can be:
    - how it is done with photos of the process, or a specific material. These guys sell 5000-USD knifes made with meteorite for example : https://www.couteau.com/
    - support local artisans doing it (marketing angle: support local community / fair pricing - angle abused by coffee distributors, but in jewelry not that much).
    - the story between the symbol (marketing angle: education about the symbols)
    - something unique: auctions are the best example of that. But you can make someone feel unique with a tailor-made jewel (see Pandora marketing), or by having them replying to some questions to determine what would be the best piece for them. I did that for supplements (based on health conditions /age / sex) and Bach flowers too.

    People need a reason to justify why they are buying something. And most men buying jewels for women need you to give them the reason why they choose this piece for her.
    First earrings I gifted to my girlfriend were small delicate 5-6mm hexagons. Hexagon is the shape of France. That's is what I told her and she loved it.

    So:
    - what do you want your brand to be focused on?
    - who do you want your brand to appeal to?
    - what is unique in your brand that people won't find elsewhere?

    For all these reasons, I won't do Amazon for jewelry personally. Amazon has a formated way of selling products that I don't think is the best for these types of product.
    Unless you want to do mass-market and compete on price, but if I had to do mass-market, I will do something people needs all year round, not gifts.

    There are so many ways to skin a cat... you need to pick a product type, then think about how to make it stand out.
